Abstract

Scarcity of action data severely limits the generalization of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models. While Latent Action Models (LAMs) address this by extracting representations from large-scale videos, their capacity for fine-grained proprioceptive control and robust generalization remains largely unquantified. To bridge this gap, we introduce the Latent Action Representation Yielding (LARY) Benchmark, a unified framework for evaluating latent action models on both low-level robotic actions and high-level semantic actions. The comprehensively curated dataset encompasses over one million short videos (totaling >1,000 hours) spanning 151 kinds of actions, alongside 620K image pairs and 595K motion trajectories, covering diverse embodiments and environments. Our experiment reveals two key insights: (i) General visual foundation models consistently outperform specialized embodied LAMs. (ii) Latent-based visual space is better aligned to robotic action space than pixel-based space. These demonstrate that robust latent actions naturally emerge from large-scale visual pre-training, and aligning control policies with the general visual features may be a promising way to VLA generalization.

Benchmark

📌Overview

Given a sequence of visual observations \( o_{1:T} \), a Latent Action Model (LAM) extracts motion information as latents \( z \in \mathcal{Z} \). LARY evaluates the efficacy of \( \mathcal{Z} \) through two complementary tasks:

\( f_{\text{sem}} : \mathcal{Z} \rightarrow \mathcal{C} \)    (Semantic Action Classification)     \( f_{\text{dyn}} : \mathcal{Z} \rightarrow \mathcal{A} \)    (Physical Action Regression)

The dataset covers 151 action categories, over 1.2M video clips (>1,000 hours), 620K image pairs, and 595K motion trajectories, spanning 11 robotic embodiments and diverse real-world environments.

📊Dataset Statistics

LARY is curated from a diverse collection of human and robot manipulation datasets, covering egocentric and exocentric perspectives across simulated and real-world environments. The dataset spans 11 robotic embodiments and includes both atomic kinematic primitives and complex composite behaviors.

Left: Dataset composition. Right: Video duration distribution across all clips.

🏷️Action Taxonomy

The benchmark defines a unified taxonomy of 151 action categories — 28 atomic kinematic primitives and 123 composite behaviors — covering a wide spectrum from frequent everyday actions (e.g., "pick", "place") to long-tail operations (e.g., "shovel", "float").

🖼️Dataset Examples

The three classification sub-tasks cover different granularities and embodiments. Atomic Robot captures fine-grained kinematic primitives from exo-centric robot demonstrations. Composite Human covers complex multi-step behaviors from egocentric human videos. Composite Robot covers task-level robot manipulation across diverse platforms.

Key Findings

💡Finding 1: General Vision Encoders Outperform Embodied LAMs

General vision foundation models (V-JEPA 2: 76.62% Acc, DINOv3: 0.19 MSE) substantially outperform specialized Embodied LAMs (LAPA/UniVLA/villa-X: 18–21% Acc, 0.87–0.97 MSE). This demonstrates that robust latent action representations naturally emerge from large-scale visual self-supervised pre-training, even without explicit motion supervision.

💡Finding 2: Latent-Based Visual Space Aligns Better with Robotic Action Space

Among generative encoders, latent-based models (V-JEPA 2, DINOv3) significantly outperform pixel-based models (Wan2.2, FLUX.2-dev) on regression tasks. This supports the hypothesis that actions can be more effectively decoded from latent feature space than from pixel space, suggesting a more agile path to general robotic control.

💡Finding 3: General LAMs Bridge the Gap

Our proposed General LAMs, which apply the LAPA self-supervised training paradigm on top of general vision backbones, consistently outperform Embodied LAMs. LAPA-DINOv2 achieves 49.36% Acc vs. UniVLA's 17.99%, despite sharing the same backbone — demonstrating that more diverse training data and fewer task-specific constraints are key to generalization.

💡Finding 4: Effective LAM Design Requires Robust Priors and Stable Quantization

Ablation studies reveal two critical design factors for effective latent action models:

  • Self-supervised contrastive priors (e.g., DINOv3) consistently outperform reconstruction-based and vision-language contrastive encoders for capturing fine-grained spatial-temporal correspondences.
  • Quantization bottleneck stability is essential — a moderate codebook size (64), sufficient sequence length (49), and latent dimension (256) achieve the best capacity-stability balance. Short sequences cause codebook collapse (1.6% utilization), while oversized codebooks yield diminishing returns.
